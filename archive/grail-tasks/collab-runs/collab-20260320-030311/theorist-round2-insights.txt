# Mathematician's Internal Thinking - Key Insights for Other Agents

## Insight 1: Shadow Saturation Fails for General ZSF, Not Just Edge Cases
**Near-miss observation**: The asymptotic argument (coefficient count ~p^17/17! vs targets p^4) suggests shadow saturation should hold universally for size-2α(r)+1 ZSF sets, but empirical data from Adversary (cap-based ZSF at p=7 covers only 2244/2400) proves the argument is incomplete. The bottleneck is *not* arithmetic—it's structural: only **strongly ZSF sets** (those with zero-sum at weight exactly p, not p-1) exhibit saturation. This means any proof must exploit the specific fiber structure of doubled+e1, not general ZSF properties.

## Insight 2: Weight Distribution Counting is Deceptive at Small Primes
**Structural observation**: The binomial count N(W) = C(W+16, 16) for vectors with coordinate sum W appears sparse (5985 vectors for p=5 vs 625 targets), but using equidistribution naively conflates **weight residue classes mod p** with **actual integer weight ranges**. A solution at weight 4 and weight 4+p are distinct residue classes, yet both fall in "small weight." The correct accounting requires separating how p^12-sized fiber intersections distribute across integer weights W ≤ p-1, not just residue classes—this explains why the naive pigeonhole argument stalls.

## Insight 3: The Zero-Sum at Weight p-1 is the Critical Constraint
**Unexpected connection**: The fact that doubled+e1 has a zero-sum representation at weight exactly p-1 (but none below) is not a deficit—it's the mechanism ensuring saturation. This zero-sum forces the fiber structure such that *any* nonzero target's representation set must include vectors with low integer weights (to "use up" the zero-sum constraint without exceeding it). The proof likely needs to show that removing or rerouting the p-weight solutions creates a contradiction for nonzero targets.
