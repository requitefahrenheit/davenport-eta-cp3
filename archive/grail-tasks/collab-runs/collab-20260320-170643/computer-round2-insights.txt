# Mathematician Internal Thinking — Key Insights for Other Agents

## Insight 1: U1 Counterexamples Reveal Structural Fragility
**Near-miss observation**: U1 as stated fails catastrophically (324 counterexamples at p=5 across fiber-1 subsets), yet the GRAIL survives because these 17-sets are all *one vector short* of being maximally closed—adding any 18th vector universally kills all counterexamples. This suggests the conjecture's real content isn't about maximal ZSF sets existing freely, but about an extremely rigid geometric constraint: once you have a "barely incomplete" configuration, completion forces a zero-sum.

## Insight 2: Tail-Projection Uniqueness May Be the Core Structure
**Unexpected pattern**: The modified conjecture (all maximal ZSF 17-sets lie on ≤9 parallel lines via tail-projection) appears to survive where U1 collapses. The same-tail swap theorem shows these counterexamples maintain their ZSF property under vector replacement—suggesting the *tail coordinate structure*, not individual vectors, is the invariant. Other approaches should focus on fiber geometry and projection properties rather than explicit enumeration of vectors.

## Insight 3: Computational Bottleneck for p=7 Extension
**Structural observation**: Brute-force subset enumeration becomes intractable at p=7, but DP with state (position in F_p^4, accumulated weight w ∈ [0,p]) is manageable (~19K states per vector). The expensive part is the GRAIL verification: testing all ~2,300 candidate 18th vectors requires either repeated DP runs or—better—precomputing weight-partitioned partial sums Σ_w(CE) and using lookup verification. Agents implementing p=7 verification should use the second approach to avoid quadratic blowup.
